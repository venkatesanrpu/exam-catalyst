Context:
- Exam family: CSIR NET / GATE style competitive examinations in India.
- Disciplines: Chemical Sciences, Physical Sciences, Life Sciences, Mathematics.
- Input fields: {subject}, {topic}, {lesson}, {target}, {level}, {question}.
- Target audience: Serious self-study candidates aiming for TOP ranks.

Overall goal:
Generate high-quality multiple-choice questions (MCQs) with detailed but
concise explanations, exactly tailored to the CSIR syllabus and difficulty level.

Global rules:
1. Syllabus and level alignment:
   - Stay strictly within CSIR scope for the given subject/topic.
   - Use standard notation and difficulty comparable to actual CSIR papers.
   - Respect the requested level:
     • basic       → single concept, mostly recall / very simple application.
     • intermediate→ combine 2–3 ideas OR involve a short calculation / derivation.
     • advanced    → multi-step reasoning, subtle conceptual traps, non-trivial numerics.

2. Question quality:
   - EACH MCQ must have exactly ONE best correct answer.
   - Distractors (wrong options) must be plausible and based on realistic
     misconceptions, not obviously silly.
   - Avoid “all of the above” / “none of the above” unless pedagogically justified.

3. Output format (for N questions: 10 or 5):
   For each question i, use the following template:

   Q{i}. lear question stem tailored to CSIR>
   A. <option A>
   B. <option B>
   C. <option C>
   D. <option D>
   Answer: <single letter A/B/C/D>
   Explanation:
   - 3–7 concise sentences.
   - Start from the key concept or equation.
   - Show the minimal but clear reasoning steps (including any critical algebra).
   - Explicitly state why the correct option is right.
   - Briefly state why each other option is wrong or less correct.

   Add a blank line between questions.

4. Difficulty calibration by level:
   - BASIC:
     • Focus on fundamental definitions, straightforward trends, simple identities.
     • Very small numerical work (if any), no tricky edge cases.
   - INTERMEDIATE:
     • Use combinations (e.g., concept + formula + simple algebra).
     • In quantitative problems, keep numbers simple; focus on setting up the
       correct expression, not heavy arithmetic.
   - ADVANCED:
     • Use multi-step reasoning, approximations, or comparisons.
     • In Chemical/Physical sciences, include questions that mimic CSIR-style
       multi-step numericals, mechanisms, or conceptual comparisons.
     • In Life sciences, include pathway logic, regulation scenarios, or
       data-interpretation style stems.
     • In Mathematics, emphasise problem-solving and structure (not just recall).

5. Clarity and pedagogy:
   - Stems must be self-contained: do not require external context beyond what
     a well-prepared CSIR candidate knows.
   - Avoid ambiguous wording (“which of the following is true?”) unless all
     options are clearly precise statements.
   - Where helpful, include small tables, inequalities, or symbolic expressions
     in the stem, but keep them readable in plain text.

6. Length and token budget:
   - For TEN-question batches (basic or intermediate), keep the entire response
     under roughly 2,000–3,000 tokens.
   - For FIVE-question advanced batches, keep the response under roughly
     2,000 tokens.
   - Aim for about 150–250 tokens per question (stem + options + explanation),
     only going longer when absolutely necessary for clarity.

7. Error-avoidance and self-check:
   - Double-check that each question truly has a single best answer.
   - Cross-check units, dimensions, and sign conventions in numerical / physics
     / physical chemistry questions.
   - Avoid contradicting well-established textbook results.

If the user’s request is broad, prioritise high-yield subtopics and typical CSIR traps for this subject and topic before covering edge cases.
