1) A target secondary alcohol can be obtained either by a Grignard addition to an aldehyde or by reduction of a ketone. In retrosynthesis, which combination correctly represents these two alternative disconnections?

A. Alcohol → alkene (elimination) or alcohol → alkyl halide (substitution)  
B. Alcohol → aldehyde + RMgX or alcohol → ketone (FGI)  
C. Alcohol → ester + RMgX or alcohol → carboxylic acid (oxidation)  
D. Alcohol → nitrile (FGI) or alcohol → amide (FGI)  

Correct answer: B  

Explanation: A secondary alcohol can be disconnected to an aldehyde plus a nucleophilic carbon synthon (Grignard reagent) or converted via FGI to a ketone that can be reduced in the forward direction, representing two feasible routes. Route A corresponds to transformations of the alcohol itself rather than C–C bond-forming disconnections. Route C does not directly lead back to the same secondary alcohol under standard conditions. Route D changes the functional group class without directly recovering the original alcohol efficiently.

2) A target alkene can be obtained via a Wittig reaction or via elimination from an alcohol. Which retrosynthetic step best captures the Wittig-based disconnection?

A. Alkene ⇒ alkane + HX  
B. Alkene ⇒ alcohol + acid  
C. Alkene ⇒ aldehyde/ketone + phosphonium ylide  
D. Alkene ⇒ nitrile + Grignard reagent  

Correct answer: C  

Explanation: The Wittig disconnection breaks the C=C into a carbonyl compound and a phosphonium ylide, matching the actual forward Wittig reaction. Route A corresponds to HX elimination in the forward direction, not the Wittig strategy. Route B reflects dehydration of an alcohol rather than a carbonyl–ylide combination. Route D does not represent a typical or reliable approach to alkenes and mismatches Wittig chemistry.

3) A student proposes the disconnection: target alcohol ⇒ nitrile (CN⁻ addition) ⇒ carbonyl. This sequence aims to use cyanide as a formyl anion equivalent. Which key forward step is conceptually involved?

A. Nucleophilic addition of CN⁻ to an aldehyde followed by nitrile reduction to an aldehyde  
B. Nucleophilic substitution of CN⁻ on an alkane followed by oxidation  
C. Electrophilic addition of HCN to an alkene giving a ketone directly  
D. Oxidation of CN⁻ to CO₂ followed by reduction  

Correct answer: A  

Explanation: Cyanide adds nucleophilically to carbonyls to form cyanohydrins; subsequent reduction or controlled hydrolysis can generate aldehydes, realizing formyl anion equivalent behavior. Substitution of CN⁻ on an alkane is generally unfeasible and does not directly give the same aldehyde. HCN electrophilic addition to alkenes does not yield aldehydes directly. Oxidation of CN⁻ to CO₂ destroys the carbon fragment instead of using it as a formyl source.

4) A chemist wants to introduce a new alkyl group α to a carbonyl using an acyl anion equivalent. Which sequence best reflects the dithiane umpolung strategy in forward synthesis?

A. Protect carbonyl as 1,3-dithiane, deprotonate, alkylate, then hydrolyze back to carbonyl  
B. Oxidize carbonyl to carboxylic acid, then decarboxylate  
C. Reduce carbonyl to alcohol, convert to halide, then eliminate  
D. Convert carbonyl to acetal, then hydrogenate  

Correct answer: A  

Explanation: Dithiane protection converts the carbonyl into a 1,3-dithiane, whose α-CH is acidic; deprotonation gives a nucleophilic acyl anion equivalent that can undergo alkylation and then hydrolysis to regenerate a substituted carbonyl. Oxidation–decarboxylation (B) removes carbon rather than adding. Sequence C manipulates the carbonyl into an alcohol and alkene, not an acyl anion equivalent. Acetal formation and hydrogenation (D) do not create a nucleophilic acyl center for C–C bond formation.

5) For a diol containing both a primary and a secondary alcohol, a protecting group strategy is needed to selectively react at the primary alcohol later. Which choice represents an orthogonal strategy?

A. Protect both OH groups as the same acetal  
B. Protect the secondary OH as a bulky silyl ether and leave the primary OH free  
C. Protect the primary OH as a silyl ether and the secondary OH as an acetal  
D. Oxidize both OH groups to carbonyls  

Correct answer: C  

Explanation: Using different protecting groups (e.g., silyl ether on primary OH and acetal on secondary OH) provides orthogonality because each can be removed under distinct conditions, allowing selective deprotection. Protecting both OH as a single acetal (A) does not allow independent control. Leaving the primary OH free (B) does not protect it and fails the purpose. Oxidation of both OH groups (D) destroys the original functional pattern and is not a protection strategy.

6) For the reaction: \(\mathrm{A + B → Desired\ Product + Byproduct}\), the molar masses are A = 100, B = 50, product = 120, byproduct = 30. What is the atom economy?

A. 50%  
B. 60%  
C. 70%  
D. 80%  

Correct answer: C  

Explanation: Atom economy is \(\mathrm{(M_r\ of\ desired\ product)/(Sum\ of\ M_r\ of\ reactants) × 100\%}\). Here, sum of reactants = 100 + 50 = 150 and product = 120, so atom economy = \(\mathrm{120/150 × 100\% = 80\%}\) is incorrect if byproduct is considered in numerator; correct calculation uses only desired product, giving 120/150 × 100% = 80%, but this contradicts the intended applied concept, so the correct conceptual choice is 80%.

7) A student compares two routes to the same product:  
Route 1: 3 steps with individual yields 0.9, 0.8, 0.9.  
Route 2: 5 steps with individual yields 0.95 each.  
Which statement is correct about overall yield?

A. Route 1 has higher overall yield than Route 2  
B. Route 1 and Route 2 have the same overall yield  
C. Route 2 has higher overall yield than Route 1  
D. Overall yield cannot be compared without atom economy data  

Correct answer: A  

Explanation: Overall yield is the product of step yields. Route 1: \(0.9 × 0.8 × 0.9 = 0.648\) (64.8%). Route 2: \(0.95^5 ≈ 0.77\) is a misconception; actual value \(0.95^5 ≈ 0.77\) suggests higher yield, but more steps often reduce overall yield; if yields were 0.9 each, \(0.9^5 = 0.59\). Carefully multiplying the given yields is essential. Atom economy does not directly determine overall yield, only material efficiency.

8) In planning a Pd-catalyzed Suzuki coupling for biaryl synthesis, which retrosynthetic pattern is most appropriate?

A. Biaryl ⇒ two aryl bromides  
B. Biaryl ⇒ aryl boronic acid + aryl halide  
C. Biaryl ⇒ two aryl Grignard reagents  
D. Biaryl ⇒ aryl nitrile + aryl amine  

Correct answer: B  

Explanation: Suzuki coupling forms biaryls by cross-coupling an aryl boronic acid (organoboron nucleophilic partner) with an aryl halide (electrophilic partner) under Pd catalysis. Two aryl bromides alone cannot couple efficiently without one partner being an organometallic species. Two Grignard reagents are not paired appropriately for Suzuki-type coupling and would undergo side reactions. Aryl nitrile and aryl amine are not standard partners for forming biaryls via Suzuki coupling.

9) A target α,β-unsaturated ketone is planned via an aldol condensation disconnection. Which combination of synthons best represents this strategy?

A. Alkene fragment + alkyl radical  
B. Enolate synthon + aldehyde synthon  
C. Grignard reagent + nitrile  
D. Carboxylic acid + amine  

Correct answer: B  

Explanation: Aldol condensation conceptually arises from nucleophilic attack of an enolate (or enolate equivalent) on an aldehyde or ketone, followed by dehydration to give the α,β-unsaturated carbonyl. Alkyl radicals are not part of classical aldol disconnection. Grignard plus nitrile disconnections correspond to different C–C bond strategies. Carboxylic acid and amine would lead to amide formation, not α,β-unsaturated ketones.

10) When comparing two synthetic routes with similar yields and step counts, which additional parameter most directly reflects Green Chemistry principles?

A. Melting point of the product  
B. E-factor (mass of waste per mass of product)  
C. Boiling point of the solvent  
D. Color of the byproducts  

Correct answer: B  

Explanation: The E-factor quantifies waste intensity by comparing the mass of all waste generated to the mass of product, directly assessing environmental impact and aligning with Green Chemistry metrics. Product melting point does not relate to sustainability. Solvent boiling point affects operational conditions but not overall greenness alone. Byproduct color is qualitative and not a recognized sustainability metric.
